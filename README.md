# Strong_Password_and_Evaluate_Its_Strength

A strong password is one that's hard for attackers to guess or crack using brute-force methods, dictionary attacks, or rainbow tables. Key factors include:

Length: At least 12-16 characters (longer is better).
Complexity: Mix of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special symbols (e.g., !@#$%).
Uniqueness: Not reused across accounts; avoid personal info like names or birthdays.
Randomness: Not based on common words or patterns.
Avoid weak practices like "password123" or sequential keys. Use a password manager for generation and storage.

Example of a Strong Password

Why it's strong: 24 characters long, includes uppercase/lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols. It's not a dictionary word or phrase, and it incorporates randomness (e.g., inspired by a pangram but altered).
Evaluating Its Strength
To assess strength, use tools like password checkers (e.g., from Have I Been Pwned or online entropy calculators). For this example:

Length: 24 characters – Excellent (exceeds minimum recommendations).
Character Variety: Uppercase (T, Q, B, F), lowercase (r, v, e, etc.), numbers (4, 3, 1, 9, 9), symbols (!). – High complexity.
Entropy Estimate: Roughly 140+ bits (very high; a 12-character random password has ~70 bits, making this resistant to cracking).
Potential Weaknesses: If based on a known phrase (like "The quick brown fox"), it could be vulnerable to dictionary attacks, but the modifications (numbers, symbols, capitalization) mitigate this.
Overall Rating: Very strong. It would take billions of years to brute-force with modern hardware. For real use, generate your own via a secure tool to ensure uniqueness.
